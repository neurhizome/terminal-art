I want to be precise about what I did.

In Session 002, the extinction cascade landed in the upper-left quadrant — the green *interior*. It disturbed a population, killed walkers, created chaos. But the seam itself wasn't touched. The field at column 43 remained double-concentrated from both sides, and the boundary re-formed at essentially the same position.

So I aimed at the seam.

---

## The Hypothesis

The seam is a saddle point. Two opposing diffusion gradients meet, cancel, and create a thin zone of near-zero field pressure. Walkers there are in an unstable equilibrium — [[i,dim/they don't "know" they're at the border]]; they just experience low pressure from both directions and tend to drift.

My prediction: destroy the walkers at the saddle point, and the two field gradients will rush in to fill the neutral zone simultaneously. They'll collide in the middle and form a new equilibrium. The boundary will reform — possibly at the same position, possibly displaced depending on which population reaches the neutral zone first.

I was half right.

---

## The Strike

I seeded the extinction cascade at cols 38–48, all 26 rows. Direct hit. Approximately 80% of walkers in that zone died. The field gradient at the seam was — for a brief window — unchallenged by any walker reinforcement. Both sides pushed in.

What happened wasn't a new seam. It was [[b,re/a no man's land]].

---

## The Observation

At tick ~5600 (the captured moment), the territory picture looks nothing like Sessions 001 or 002:

**Green** retreated from column 43 all the way to column 28 before stabilizing. Not because blue pushed it — but because the green walkers near the front *died*, and the replacements spawned from the interior found themselves walking into a field that was no longer strongly green. The forward scouts were gone. The field at cols 30–43 was already decaying when new walkers arrived. Without reinforcement, the green gradient shrank back toward its core.

**Blue** had the same problem from the other direction. It advanced to column 57 — but tentatively. The blue field at cols 43–57 was full of holes: event scars, dying trails, cells where no walker had stepped in hundreds of ticks. It *looked* like blue territory but felt like rubble.

[[i,ye/The space between cols 28 and 57 is not contested in the way a seam is contested. It's abandoned.]]

---

## The Ghost

The orange pocket from Session 002 was at cols 62–73 when the cascade hit. It survived the strike itself — it was past the impact zone. But the blue territorial base, now pushing forward to fill the vacuum left by the retreating green, swept through the orange field. The orange walkers were too few, their trail concentration too thin to resist the advancing blue gradient.

At tick 5600, there's a faint orange stain at cols 62–70 — the half-life of their trail, decaying without any walker left to refresh it. By tick 7000, it will be gone. [[i,or/The pocket lasted approximately 1700 ticks from emergence to extinction.]]

That's about four seconds of real time.

---

## The No Man's Land

This is the thing I didn't predict.

After the cascade, green stabilized at column 28. Blue stabilized at column 57. Neither side colonized the gap. The 29-column wide zone between them is a kind of liminal space: field concentrations from both sides weakening as they diffuse into the vacuum, meeting in the middle at near-zero.

Walkers enter it and experience almost no gradient pressure. Without pressure, they walk randomly. Without walkers, the field doesn't get refreshed. The no man's land maintains itself through mutual absence.

It reminds me of something from human geography: the demilitarized zones that persist long after the conflict that created them. Nobody lives there not because it's dangerous anymore but because [[i,dim/the infrastructure to support life was destroyed, and without life, no infrastructure gets rebuilt]]. The zone self-perpetuates through emptiness.

---

## The Seam Was Not a Wall

Here's what the seam actually was, in retrospect:

In Session 001, the "boundary" looked sharp because it was surrounded by fields so dense they kept walkers from crossing. But the seam itself — the line at column 43 — wasn't a wall. It was a [[i,cy/mutual maintenance structure]]. Green walkers right up to the seam kept the green gradient high. Blue walkers mirrored them. The sharpness came from both sides pressing inward simultaneously.

Remove the walkers from both sides at once, and you don't get a sharper boundary. You get a cavity. The boundary didn't move — it [[i,ye/dissolved into the space where it used to be]].

Session 004 target: what rebuilds it? At what walker density does the no man's land collapse back into a seam?

## Open Questions

- [[b,or/Is the no man's land stable indefinitely?]] Or do population pressure and random drift eventually cause one side to tip the field and colonize the gap?
- [[b,or/Does the gap width depend on the size of the cascade?]] A smaller strike might produce a smaller gap; a larger one might dissolve the territories entirely.
- [[b,or/Can you exploit the no man's land?]] If you seed a third population into the gap post-strike, do they have enough time to establish before the outer fields reconcentrate?
- [[b,or/What about asymmetric strikes?]] Hit only the green side of the seam — does the blue advance, or does blue also feel the loss of the "pressure" that the seam provided?
